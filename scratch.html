Wealth Management
Is a Scam.
The Math Proves It.

I study finance at BYU. Here's what the industry doesn't want you to know about the 1% you're paying your advisor.

Let me be upfront: I'm a finance student at BYU's Marriott School, I've worked in private equity, and I'm heading to Solamere Capital and Lazard. I understand the financial industry from the inside. And I'm here to tell you that the entire "wealth management" model — the guy in a nice suit who takes 1–2% of your assets per year to "manage" your money — is one of the most quietly devastating financial arrangements most middle-class Americans have ever agreed to.

This isn't a conspiracy theory. It's arithmetic. And John Oliver, of all people, explained it better than most finance professors I've sat under. But first, let's run the numbers ourselves.

Section 1

The Fee Bomb: Why 1% Is Not "Just 1%"

Your wealth manager charges 1% annually. Sounds small. It's not. Here's why: you don't pay fees on your original investment. You pay fees on your compounding returns. Every dollar that compound interest generates gets taxed again by the fee the next year.

The 30-year fee math on $100,000

$432,194

Estimated wealth destroyed by a 1% annual fee over 30 years at 7% return. Your portfolio would have grown to $761,226 without fees — you get $329,032 instead.

That's not a rounding error. That's your retirement. A 1% fee over 30 years doesn't take 1% of your wealth — it takes roughly 43% of it. John Oliver put it perfectly when he said fees are like termites: tiny, barely noticeable, and capable of eating your entire financial future.

Section 2

Your Advisor Is a Salesperson, Not Your Friend

Photo: Unsplash — The ticker boards most wealth managers are watching while charging your fee.

The financial industry has a brilliant vocabulary problem. "Wealth manager," "financial advisor," "financial consultant," "investment specialist" — these titles sound official, credentialed, trustworthy. Here's the thing: none of them are legally regulated terms. Per FINRA, anyone can call themselves any of those things.

The distinction that actually matters is whether your advisor is a fiduciary — someone legally required to act in your best interest. Most "wealth managers" working at big brokerages operate under a suitability standard instead. Suitability just means the product can't be completely insane for you — not that it's the best option. They can sell you a high-fee annuity that makes them a fat commission as long as it's not wildly inappropriate for your situation.

Section 3

The Data: Active Managers Almost Always Lose

Every year, S&P Global publishes the SPIVA (S&P Indices Versus Active) scorecard. It compares actively managed mutual funds against their relevant index benchmarks. The results are remarkably consistent: over any 15-year period, roughly 88–92% of active large-cap fund managers underperform the S&P 500.

Read that again. The people who spend their entire careers analyzing stocks, with Bloomberg terminals, research teams, and institutional access, can't beat a passive index 9 times out of 10. And yet they charge you 1–2% per year for the privilege of watching them lose.

"Over 15 years, roughly 9 out of 10 active fund managers fail to beat their benchmark — after fees."

— S&P Global SPIVA Scorecard, U.S. Large Cap Funds

Section 4

Buffett's Million-Dollar Bet That Proved All of This

In 2008, Warren Buffett made a public wager: he bet $1,000,000 that a simple Vanguard S&P 500 index fund would outperform a professionally selected portfolio of five hedge funds of funds over 10 years. Protégé Partners took the bet and selected five funds-of-funds representing dozens of underlying hedge funds managed by highly paid professionals.

The result in 2017: Buffett's index fund returned 125.8% over the period. The hedge funds averaged 36.3%. And remember — those hedge fund returns were after the hedge fund fees were already stripped out. Add them back in and the underperformance was even more extreme.

Buffett donated his winnings to charity. The lesson was free.

Section 5

What to Actually Do With Your Money

This isn't nihilism. There's a real and simple answer. Here's a practical action plan, developed from everything the data actually supports:

  1. Start with low-cost index funds immediately. The Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) and Fidelity ZERO Total Market Index (FZROX) have expense ratios of 0.03–0.00%. That is not a typo. Your "wealth manager" charging 1% is 33x to infinitely more expensive.
    • VOO: 0.03% expense ratio, tracks S&P 500
    • FZROX: 0.00% expense ratio, total US market
    • VXUS: 0.07% expense ratio, international diversification
    • BND: 0.03% expense ratio, bond index for diversification
  2. Max your tax-advantaged accounts first, in this order. Sequence matters enormously for long-term compounding.
    • 401(k) up to employer match (free money — never leave this on the table)
    • HSA if eligible (triple tax-advantaged: contributions, growth, and withdrawals)
    • Roth IRA up to the annual limit ($7,000 in 2025 if under 50)
    • Back to 401(k) up to the full contribution limit
    • Taxable brokerage account for any remaining investable income
  3. Ask your advisor point blank: "Are you a fiduciary?" If they hesitate, equivocate, or give you a non-answer, leave. If they say no, definitely leave. A suitability-standard advisor's legal obligation is to the product, not to you.
    • Look for CFP (Certified Financial Planner) with fee-only compensation
    • NAPFA.org lists fee-only fiduciary advisors by location
    • Fee-only means they charge you flat or hourly — no commission on products they sell
  4. Stop buying actively managed mutual funds. The data is overwhelming. If your current portfolio has expense ratios above 0.20%, you are paying for underperformance.
    • Check your fund's expense ratio at Morningstar.com
    • Compare its 10-year return against its benchmark index
    • In almost every case, switch to the index version and pocket the difference
  5. Do the math yourself. Don't let the complexity of financial jargon intimidate you into paying someone else to do something that takes 30 minutes to set up and then runs on autopilot.
    • Open a Vanguard, Fidelity, or Schwab account online in under 10 minutes
    • Set up automatic monthly contributions to a three-fund portfolio
    • Rebalance annually. That's the whole strategy.

None of this requires a wealth manager. None of it requires paying anyone 1% of everything you own every single year until you die. The three-fund portfolio — US stocks, international stocks, bonds — is the most evidence-backed, low-cost retirement strategy on earth. It fits in a sentence. It doesn't fit the business model of a firm charging 1.5% AUM.
