Wealth Management Is a Scam.

The math doesn't lie — and your advisor is counting on you not doing it.

Let me be upfront: I'm a finance student at BYU's Marriott School, I've worked in private equity, and I'm heading to Solamere Capital and Lazard. I understand this industry from the inside. And I'm here to tell you that the entire "wealth management" model — the guy in the nice suit who takes 1–2% of your assets every year — is one of the most quietly devastating financial arrangements most Americans have ever agreed to.

This isn't a conspiracy theory. It's arithmetic. Jump to the math ↓

1. The Fee Bomb: Why 1% Is Not "Just 1%"

Your wealth manager charges 1% annually. Sounds small. It's not. You don't pay fees on your original investment — you pay fees on your compounding returns. Every dollar that compound interest generates gets taxed again by the fee next year.

2. Your Advisor Is a Salesperson, Not Your Friend

Photo: Unsplash — The data your wealth manager hopes you never look at too closely.

The financial industry has a brilliant vocabulary problem. "Wealth manager," "financial advisor," "financial consultant," "investment specialist" — these sound official and credentialed. Per FINRA, none of those titles are legally regulated. Anyone can use them.

The distinction that actually matters is fiduciary — someone legally required to act in your best interest. Most "wealth managers" at big brokerages operate under a suitability standard instead. That just means the product can't be completely wrong for you — not that it's the best option. They can sell you a high-fee annuity that makes them a fat commission as long as it vaguely fits your situation.

3. The Data: Active Managers Almost Always Lose

Every year, S&P Global publishes the SPIVA scorecard comparing actively managed mutual funds against their index benchmarks. The results are remarkably consistent: over any 15-year period, roughly 88–92% of active large-cap fund managers underperform the S&P 500.

The people who spend their entire careers analyzing stocks, with Bloomberg terminals and research teams, can't beat a passive index fund 9 times out of 10. And they charge you 1–2% a year for the privilege of watching them lose.

4. Buffett's Million-Dollar Bet

In 2008, Warren Buffett publicly wagered $1,000,000 that a simple Vanguard S&P 500 index fund would outperform a professionally selected basket of five hedge funds of funds over 10 years. Protégé Partners took the bet.

The result in 2017: Buffett's index fund returned 125.8%. The hedge funds averaged 36.3%. And those hedge fund returns were already after their enormous fees. Buffett donated his winnings to charity. The lesson was free.

5. What to Actually Do With Your Money

None of this requires a wealth manager. Here's a simple action plan the data actually supports:

  1. Buy low-cost index funds immediately. Vanguard (VOO), Fidelity ZERO (FZROX), or Schwab (SCHB) charge 0.00–0.03% in fees. That's not a typo. Your wealth manager charging 1% is 33x more expensive for worse results.
    • VOO — Vanguard S&P 500 ETF, 0.03% expense ratio
    • FZROX — Fidelity ZERO Total Market, 0.00% expense ratio
    • VXUS — Vanguard International, 0.07% expense ratio
    • BND — Vanguard Total Bond Market, 0.03% expense ratio
  2. Max tax-advantaged accounts first, in this order. Sequence matters for compounding.
    • 401(k) up to employer match — this is free money, never leave it behind
    • HSA if eligible — triple tax advantage (contributions, growth, withdrawals)
    • Roth IRA up to the annual limit ($7,000 in 2025)
    • Back to 401(k) up to the full contribution limit
    • Taxable brokerage account for remaining investable income
  3. Ask your advisor if they are a fiduciary. If they hesitate or say no, leave. Look for a fee-only CFP at NAPFA.org.
    • Fee-only = they charge you flat or hourly, no commissions
    • CFP (Certified Financial Planner) is the most meaningful credential
    • NAPFA.org lists fee-only fiduciary advisors by zip code
  4. Stop buying actively managed mutual funds. If your fund's expense ratio is above 0.20%, you are paying for underperformance.
    • Check your fund's expense ratio at Morningstar.com
    • Compare its 10-year return against its benchmark index
    • In almost every case, switch to the index version
  5. Do the math yourself. Open a Vanguard, Fidelity, or Schwab account in 10 minutes. Set up automatic monthly contributions to a three-fund portfolio. Rebalance once a year. That's the whole strategy.
    • Three-fund portfolio: US stocks + international stocks + bonds
    • Adjust the bond percentage as you get older
    • Ignore daily market noise. Stay the course.
