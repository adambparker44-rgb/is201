Wealth Management Is a Scam.

The math proves it. Your advisor is counting on you never doing it.

I'm a Finance student at BYU's Marriott School, I've worked in private equity, and I have offers at Solamere Capital and Lazard. I understand this industry from the inside. And I'm here to tell you that the "wealth management" model — the guy in a nice suit charging 1-2% of your money every year — is one of the most quietly devastating financial arrangements most Americans have ever agreed to.

This isn't a conspiracy theory. It's arithmetic. See the math below.

1. The Fee Bomb: Why 1% Is Not "Just 1%"

Your wealth manager charges 1% annually. You don't pay fees on your original investment — you pay fees on your compounding returns. Every dollar compound interest generates gets hit by the fee again next year.

The 30-year fee math on $100,000

$432,000

Wealth destroyed by a 1% annual fee over 30 years at 7% return. Without fees you'd have ~$761,000. With the fee you get ~$329,000. That's not 1% of your wealth gone — that's 43% of it.

"Think of fees like termites. They're tiny, barely noticeable, and they can eat away your entire future."

2. Your Advisor Is a Salesperson, Not Your Friend

Photo: Unsplash — The data your wealth manager hopes you never look up yourself.

"Wealth manager," "financial advisor," "financial consultant" — these sound official. Per FINRA, none of those titles are legally regulated. Anyone can use them.

The term that matters is fiduciary — someone legally required to act in your best interest. Most wealth managers operate under a suitability standard instead, meaning a product just has to not be completely wrong for you. They can sell you a high-fee annuity that makes them a fat commission as long as it vaguely fits your situation.

3. The Data: Active Managers Almost Always Lose

S&P Global's SPIVA scorecard compares actively managed funds to their index benchmarks every year. Result: over any 15-year period, about 90% of active fund managers underperform the S&P 500. They charge 1-2% for the privilege of watching them lose.

"Over 15 years, roughly 9 out of 10 active fund managers fail to beat their benchmark — after fees."
— S&P Global SPIVA Scorecard

4. Buffett's Million-Dollar Bet

In 2008, Warren Buffett bet $1,000,000 that a simple Vanguard S&P 500 index fund would beat five professionally managed hedge funds over 10 years. Protégé Partners took the bet.

Result in 2017: Buffett's index fund returned 125.8%. The hedge funds averaged 36.3% — and those returns were already after fees. Buffett donated his winnings to charity. The lesson was free.

5. What to Actually Do With Your Money

No wealth manager required. Here's what the evidence supports:

  1. Buy low-cost index funds. Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab charge 0.00–0.05% — not a typo. Your wealth manager at 1% is up to 20x more expensive for worse results.
    • VOO — Vanguard S&P 500 ETF, 0.03%
    • FZROX — Fidelity ZERO Total Market, 0.00%
    • VXUS — Vanguard International, 0.07%
    • BND — Vanguard Total Bond Market, 0.03%
  2. Max tax-advantaged accounts in this order.
    • 401(k) up to employer match — free money, never leave it behind
    • HSA if eligible — triple tax advantage
    • Roth IRA up to the annual limit ($7,000 in 2025)
    • Back to 401(k) up to the full limit, then taxable brokerage
  3. Ask your advisor: "Are you a fiduciary?" If they hesitate or say no, leave. Find a fee-only CFP at NAPFA.org.
    • Fee-only = charges flat or hourly, no commissions
    • CFP = the credential that actually means something
  4. Check your expense ratios. If any fund is above 0.20%, switch to its index equivalent.
    • Look it up at Morningstar.com
    • Compare 10-year return vs. the benchmark — almost always underperforms
  5. Set it and forget it. Three-fund portfolio, automatic contributions, rebalance once a year. Done.
    • US stocks + international stocks + bonds
    • Shift more toward bonds as you get older
    • Ignore daily market noise
